UX case study

The Grumbler

A UX case study about making a puzzle clearer without reducing the complexity.

  • 80% 8 out of 10 players completed puzzle 4 after updating puzzle 3
  • +50% 3 out of 10 players completed puzzle 4 before improvements to puzzle 3. The study produced a 50% inprovement
  • 10 A small cohort of university student participants took part throughout the study

Abstract summary

Teaching without taking over

The Grumbler is a small indie 2D puzzle platformer about a hungry stomach monster collecting food (Omi), defeating enemies, and buying hats. Participants in the study understood the character and the basic movement quickly, but puzzle 4 was where we saw the most frustration and 'rage quitting'. The game was asking players to complete the level without the game first effectively teaching them the right lessons to succeed.

Study result

The study aimed to determine why players were becoming stuck at the same section and failing to complete the 4th puzzle. The initial assumption was that the level was too complex, trying to teach the player to chain too many concepts together at once.

The most useful change I found was updating the projectiles to include a trajectory path to indicate where the projectile would land once thrown. The previous puzzle was also streamlined by removing two types of projectiles (Black, and Blue). These would be introduced slower and in later puzzles as to not overwhelm players with multiple choices too early and give the player more opportunities to practice throwing the green projectile.

Problem statement

On the 4th puzzle players were failing to unlock a certain passageway correctly, they would end up stuck with no obvious way forward.

Objectives and goals

Help players move forward without frustration.

Player learning

Make the game communicate what skills were still needed, then reward the player with clear progression goals once they had solved the first part of each puzzle.

Progression

Get more players through puzzle 4 without reducing the complexity of the puzzle.

Production fit

Use the level objects, UI language, and art constraints that already existed, because this was a small indie game and the fix needed to be realistic.

Process outline

Turning stuck moments into design decisions

1. Diagnose

I looked at fail points, abandon points, hint toggles, time on level, repeated signs of frustration in body language, and what participants said while they were trying to work it out.

2. Model

I mapped the concepts puzzle 4 relied on and found where the earlier levels were under-teaching cause and effect.

3. Prototype

I tested contextual exit hints, stronger completion feedback, clearer hint language, and earlier foreshadowing.

4. Measure

I compared completion, solve time, hint use, and confidence between the original tutorial and the improved build.

Business challenges

The solution had to fit a small indie game with very limited time and resources.

Retention risk

Puzzle 4 was early enough that a bad experience could make players decide the game was unclear before they had seen enough of it.

Scope control

A big tutorial rebuild, or entire puzzle rework would have been the wrong answer. Improvements had to be small for time constraints.

Identity protection

The Grumbler works because it is slightly silly and concepts are supposed to be taught invisibly. The tutorial could not suddenly become a sterile onboarding system that felt like it belonged in another game.

Difficulty promise

It still needed to feel like a puzzle platformer. The goal was guidance, not reduced puzzle complexity.

Target audience

The game was designed to be played by novice and experienced puzzle platformers alike.

The recruited participant group were exclusively white Scottish university students in their early 20s, predominantly self-identified 'gamers' ranging from casual to advanced, with one non-gamer participant. They were close to the audience I expected for the game.

  • Gamers and non-gamers
  • Short-session play
  • Humor-led indie game appeal
  • Low to advanced platforming skill
  • Low tolerance for unclear failure
  • Optional help preferred over forced help

Research type

Mixed method: quantitative and qualitative

I used quantitative data to see where progression was failing, along with observation and questionnaires to understand why. I did not want to make the puzzle easier just because people were failing it. I wanted to know whether the pain points were.

Quantitative

I tracked completion rate, level duration, retries, deaths, hint toggles, exit interactions before completion, and abandon rate.

Observations

Participants kept saying the same kind of thing. They could see the goal, but they thought there was no way to achieve it at the point when they either asked for help, quit, or reported the level as broken.

Participants and their screens were recorded and viewed back to ensure I had not missed any details of their test.

Questionnaire

The pre-game questions focused on participants' exposure to PC puzzle platform games including their dexterity regarding simultaneous keyboard and mouse movements.

The post-game questions focused on fairness, confidence, remembered objectives, and whether each puzzle's lessons were learned.

User challenges

Where the puzzle lost people

Hidden dependency

The sequence depended on advanced projectile handling but those skills weren't there yet.

Help avoidance

Some players ignored the hint toggle because it felt like a setting, not help that belonged to the current puzzle.

Soft lock fails

When players failed to open the entire cave entrance, there were repeated calls that the puzzle was broken or bugged.

Unclear state

Falling down the partially open cave some players tried multiple sequences and became frustrated and confused. Previous puzzle steps needed to be much clearer.

Break in tone

Generic tutorial text would have broken the stomach-world tone. The help needed to feel like it belonged in the game by being as invisible as possible.

Decision Matrix

Do first: urgent and important

  • Trajectory guide for projectiles.
  • Remove unused black and blue projectiles from puzzle 3.
  • Add more opportunities to practise green projectiles in puzzle 3.

Schedule: important, less urgent

  • Track projectile and block progress in Puzzle 4.
  • Immediate failed message with restart puzzle option.
  • Make jumping down the obvious and only answer.

Delegate: urgent, lower impact

  • Small visual polish pass for hint bubbles.
  • Text proofreading across tutorial levels.
  • Move restart level sign from top of puzzle 4 to the same level as cave entrance.

Eliminate: lower urgency and impact

  • Secret cave exit for stuck players.
  • Large redesign of puzzle 4.

5 Whys analysis graph

This was part of the root cause analysis. I kept asking why the previous answer was happening until the problem stopped being "players are bad at the puzzle" and became "I am not teaching them correctly."

Problem

Players are not completing the game

Why?

Because they are rage quitting on puzzle 4

Why?

Because they are getting frustrated and think the game is broken

Why?

Because they are getting trapped in the cave

Why?

Because they are not throwing the projectiles at the right areas

Root cause

Because there's no feedback showing where the projectiles will land

Task flows

Two flows: getting stuck and getting unstuck

Scenario 1: Original flow

1The player enters puzzle 3 and sees 1 green, 1 black, and 1 blue projectile and a green enemy.
2The player can fire a projectile at the green enemy but this is not required.
3The player completes puzzle 3 and enters puzzle 4.
4The player sees 1 green projectile and 3 green blocks.
5The player fires green projectiles at blocks with a high likelihood of missing multiple blocks.

Scenario 2: Improved flow

1The player enters puzzle 3 and sees green projectiles and green blocks.
2The player is required to fire projectiles at blocks. There is a visible trajectory guide when a projectile is armed.
3The player is required to fire green projectiles at green enemies. No other colour blocks or projectiles are used yet.
4The player completes puzzle 3 and enters puzzle 4.
5The player sees 1 green projectile within reach and 3 green blocks to destroy. They see 2 additional green projectiles past the green blocks.

Designs / Prototypes

Outcome: clearer learning, faster progress

This study found that Puzzle 4 was not too difficult. Instead, the tutorial was asking players to use skills they had not yet learned with enough confidence. The redesign focused on teaching those skills earlier by simplifying Puzzle 3 and adding clearer projectile feedback. As a result, more players completed Puzzle 4 and continued through the rest of the game without reducing the intended challenge.

The solution was not to make the puzzle easier, but to teach it better.
