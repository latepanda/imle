UX case study / July 2019 playtest

The untitled Late Panda board game

Imagine a competitive team-based board game, where players sometimes change allegiances and the game board changes each game. Playtesting is essential to get the ruleset clear without being verbose. I observed nine groups learn the game from the rulebook and play ten games across four playtest days, capturing a series of data points that helped refine the book.

01 / The game

Friends close, enemies closer.

The untitled Late Panda board game (TULP-BG) is a competitive sci-fi game. Players wake up on an alien ship on one of two teams. Each team wants control of the ship, while each player has a private goal to complete in addition to their team's goal. I treated the board, cards and tokens as one physical interface: players need to read the state quickly and readjust every time the state changes.

Deprogrammed

These are the humans resisting the aliens. They reveal rooms, complete scenarios and try to overrun the ship to get back to earth. They can also capture and reverse the indoctrination of the opposing team members.

Resist

Indoctrinated

These humans believe the aliens are helping humanity. They inspect rooms in private, set traps and try to recruit the opposing members' players to their cause.

Convert

The part I wanted to test

  • A player's team isn't fixed. Lose a fight and you may end up joining the other side. These are high stakes. You and your team members are incentivised to keep you on board.
  • If a player loses a fight, the player also loses their goal, their starting items, and the rooms they've so far captured. Their definition of success changes as they are given a new personal goal to complete. Their previous team also loses morale. If all morale is lost that team loses the game.
  • The ship is modular, with room scenarios, traps and five item decks. The board presents a different challenge each time it is played.
  • Players can be on the winning team yet still lose the game if they haven't completed their personal goal. You need to pass the two states to win.
  • I needed to determine how clear these rules were to set up and follow throughout the game. Whenever a rule was questioned or misinterpreted I was available to correct and note for future revisions. I would only step in if players were unable to determine the correct rules themselves based on the rulebooks they were given.

Ship shape early prototypes

These photographs document one of the first paper prototypes in March 2019. I used hexagonal tiles as they are the most flexible and interesting geometric shape to use for this sort of thing. There was no fixed board plan: players chose the shape of the ship each time. Moving the rooms changed the routes through the ship and kept exploration unpredictable.

02 / How it plays

A quick guide

Accessibility from the beginning

Despite the game being printed on a personal printer with basic assets I made sure every game tile, item card, scenario card, morale token and game mechanic could be played by people with visual impairments.

Each asset includes more than 1 cue to convey the same information. colours, descriptive text and icons. These assets are very early prototypes, while future prototypes may look more visually interesting, the basis of inclusivity will remain always.

01

Lay out the ship

Players build the board from hexagonal room tiles. Four starting rooms are face up at the beginning.

02

Get a side and a goal

Each player starts as Indoctrinated or Deprogrammed. They receive a personal goal and take the items printed on that card.

03

Search the rooms

Players move, search and complete scenarios. Along the way they draw Weapon, Health, Companion, Alien Tech and Trap cards.

04

Take control or pick a fight

Players can set and disarm traps, complete room scenarios, use card effects or attack somebody else.

05

Losing can change your team

A defeated player removes one morale from their old team, discards their goal and items, then joins the opposing side with a new goal.

On your turn

Draw an item card. You can then move to an adjacent room, take one action and use a bonus action if you have one, in whichever order makes sense. An item card can also be discarded for its movement value.

How winning works

A personal win requires two things: completing your own goal and either removing the opposing team or emptying its morale. If your team finishes the game but your goal is incomplete, you haven't personally won.

03 / The playtest

Four days of fun

Nine groups and 35 first-time players played ten games over four playtest days. We gave each group a copy of the rulebook and asked them to teach themselves. My partner and I stayed available for questions, and we wrote down every time somebody needed us to clarify a rule.

On Day 3, one four-player group played a second game. That replay brought the total to ten games, but it was not recorded. I omitted it from the data, graph, tables, win rates and percentages. Its four players are counted once in the 35-person participant total, and each participant contributed no more than one questionnaire response.

What I was watching for

  • Could players use the rulebook to follow a turn and work out which actions were available?
  • Was room ownership clear? Did they understand traps and notice what changed when somebody switched teams?
  • After the board changed, did each player still see a possible route to winning?
  • When the game ended, did they actually want another go?

How I ran the four days

My partner and I observed the physical games, used one paper log per game and followed up with questionnaires for each participant after their games had ended. The groups led their own play from the rulebooks provided and were encouraged to use these books to find answers themselves.

Occasionally my partner and I were required to confirm rules and answer questions to avoid games from stalling. We logging each interruption.

We recorded on paper the course of each game, where players started, when players switched teams and goals, and when milestones were crossed.

06 / The awkward maths

The preceived value and cost per unit.

After market analysis and research the lowest estimate on the table for a bulk order of 5,000 units works out to £13 per unit to produce. The total investment for a production run of this scale is £65,000. This is before marketing and distribution costs are calculated.

The cost problem

At £11, the selling price is lower than the production cost. At £20, only £7 remains before paying for distribution. This made reducing the manufacturing cost part of the design work, not a problem to leave until the end. According to the data from the playtest the game is not yet commercially viable.

Reducing the quantity of room tiles, item cards, and scenarios is an option that we would not wish to take if we could avoid it.

The next phase pairs gameplay improvements with a more realistic production plan.

07 / Notes from playtesters

Constructive feedback

Participants were encouraged to take their own notes throughout and at the end of their games. Some common themes started emerging from their feedback once analysed as a whole.

The areas of concern were areas I had initially assumed would be the most problematic which was now validated by others.

Creating and disarming traps

Several notes mention issues with trap cards. Trap cards show items that are received once the trap has been disarmed and a skill type and value needed to build the trap itself. Page 13 of the rulebook meantions traps, building traps, setting traps in you're an indoc player, setting traps if you're a deprog player, and disarming traps.

It's easy to miss at the bottom of the page the words "Disarming a trap acts just like a room scenario (Scenarios p12).

Showing room ownership

When a player owns a room they have posession of the corresponding room card. When switching teams it can get a little confusing and slow down the game trying to figure out which player and which team how owns which room. This was brought up a few times by players.

Fights

Fights happen in rounds. A single attacker places a weapon card down and targets another individual player. That weapon card has a value e.g. 3. The defender uses a health card with value equal or higher to 3. Then they place a weapon card down to attack back.

The order should then be red, green, red, green, red, green. Until the fight is won by a player. A few players were using exclusively red cards while others were fighting as whole teams.

08 / What I can claim

How the sessions shaped the next version

A stronger base to build from

The response to the game was encouraging, and the paper logs showed where the rules and physical components slowed people down. I left with clear changes to make and a better way to record the next round.

More room to test the idea

An online prototype opens the game to a broader group and makes it easier to run sessions at different player counts. Fully autonomous sessions also gives the revised instructions a proper test without us beside the table.

How I used the result

This was an early test with volunteers, and we answered questions when groups got stuck. That made it useful for finding problems in the rules and preparing a broader, unmoderated round.

09 / What I'm doing next

Plans after the sessions

01

Move the logs off paper

The paper sheets worked, but comparing nine recorded public games took time. One structured record per game makes the next round much easier to review.

02

Build the free Tabletop Simulator version

A free online prototype takes the game beyond a single physical event and helps us find players for later tests.

03

Run a test without us in the room

These groups led their own play with us available for questions. The next group gets the revised rulebook and no live help.

04

Rework the cost and route to market

A fresh production estimate gives me something useful to compare with the pricing answers. From there, I can explore publishing or licensing the game.

Why Tabletop Simulator comes first

A free online version makes the next test cheaper and opens it to more people. It also gives the revised rulebook space to stand on its own.

The ability to tinker with gameplay and release rapid prototypes would be hugely beneficial as well.

11 / Conclusion

Conclusion

The playtests gave me a strong reason to keep developing the game. Across four days, 35 first-time players learned from the rulebook, and 32 said they would play again. One four-player group even stayed for a second game, which felt like a lovely vote of confidence.

The sessions also gave me a focused list of improvements. Clearer trap instructions, stronger room-ownership cues and a simpler explanation of fights should make the game easier to learn without losing its unpredictability or team-switching tension.

The production estimate introduced a useful design challenge. With most respondents expecting a price between £11 and £20, the next version needs to be enjoyable to play and more economical to manufacture. Revising the components, testing through Tabletop Simulator and running sessions without live support will help me explore both.

I finished the playtests with encouraging feedback, practical evidence and plenty of ideas for the next prototype. The game had found an audience; my job now was to make it easier to learn, easier to test and realistic to produce.
